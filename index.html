Overview

The objective of the research is to investigate the design and fabrication of bio-based plastic structures using additive manufacturing techniques, with a focus on chitosan based hydrogels.

Towards More Sustainable Materials

In Architectural Design as well as in many other fields there is a growing interest in working with locally available sustainable materials. The amount of plastic waste is expected to exceed that of the fishes in the seas by 2050. Designing water-degradable plastics made of chitosan is one alternative. This material is derived from the chitin, a component extracted from the exoskeletons of insects or shellfish.

Context

This project is conducted as a research project in the framework of the Design by Data program in Paris, in collaboration with the multidisciplinary practice Noumena in Barcelon.

Objective of the research

The objective of this research is to investigate the design and fabrication of bio-based plastic structures, with a focus on chitosan-based hydrogels. Tasks include:

  • Definition of an experimental protocol
  • Testing of mixes in different conditions to investigate manufacturability, and control of shape and mechanical properties
  • Modelling and simulation of the final structure
  • Definition of a digital fabrication strategy and prototyping

Preliminary Tests using Bioplastics

This first phase focuses on exploring the mixing and printing process of bioplastics made with materials easily available on the market (cornstarch, gelatin, glycerin, vinegar) and chitosan. This includes understanding the sensitivity of the final shapes to temperature, stirring time, etc. Only simple shapes will be made at this stage.

The first series of tests aimed at understanding the design process of bioplastics and its main technical difficulties.

Series 1 - Cornstarch mix

  • Manual stirring of a mix of cornstarch and acid acetic.
  • Test of diﬀerent concentrations of cornstarch.
  • Adaptation of the temperature and stirring speed to obtain an extrudable paste.
  • Samples never dried.

Series 2 - Cornstarch mix + Chitosan mix

  • Testing of new concentrations of cornstarch.
  • First tests using chitosan.
  • Improvement of the experimental protocol : use of a magnetic stirrer, better control of temperature.
  • Corsntach tests was a success. The obtained paste was extrudable and allowed drawing a grid.
  • First chitosan test was a fail as the mix ended up in a non homogeneous thick paste.

Preliminary conclusions and way forward

Two different types of bioplastics were tested, one using cornstarch and the other using chitosan. Shapes were drawn with more or less success using syringes of different nozzle sizes. The first results highlighted the extreme sensitivity of both the final mix consistency and the final dried shape to the concentrations of the different materials used, the operating temperature and the process. A lot of technical issues have been encountered during the tests. Tackling them allowed improving significantly the process. Now that the experimental protocol is mature, the next step is to focus on two types of chitosan mixes, optimize their design regarding their final consistency and then quantify and model the deformation of the final shapes.

Chitosan Tests : printability, controlling shape and mechanical properties

The objective of this phase is to explore the printability of chitosan mixes. Obtaining an extrudable paste with chitosan-based materials is the main challenge of the research. The effect of the amount of glycerol and acid acetic is also investigated. The mechanical properties of these bioplastics depend significantly on the initial concentration of chitosan in the solution. Shape control, especially of shrinkage, require a specific focus.

Four sets of experiments have been conducted, with the objective to test different mixes (concentrations, additives, fillers) and different test conditions (volumes, operating temperature, etc). They also allowed reﬁning the experimental protocol through an iterative process.

Chitosan Series 1 - Chitosan test

  • Improvement of the experimental protocol : continuous progressive pouring of the chitosan powder, adaptation of the stirrer speed and the temperature.
  • Generation of various simple shapes.
  • Pouring in containers of different sizes.
  • Samples dried after several week. The drying time was very dependent from the volume of the sample. Significant shrinkage occured in all the samples.

Chitosan Series 2 - Chitosan mixes at diﬀerent concentrations

  • Improvement of the experimental protocol : use of a water bath, control of mix evaporation.
  • Testing of higher concentration of chitosan in the mix.
  • Quantiﬁcation of shrinkage on samples of diﬀerent concentrations and different sizes.
  • Generation of a larger sample.

Chitosan Series 3 - Chitosan mixes with glycerol, overhead stirrer

  • Improvement of the experimental protocol : no water bath, reduced temperature to avoid evaporation, use of an overhead stirrer.
  • High volume mixing (400 mL).
  • Same mixes as for series 2 with excess amount of glycerol.
  • The overhead stirrer allows a more regular stirring than the magnetic but the speed is much lower. Manual stirring after each introduction of chitosan can be necessary.
  • Glycerol makes the mixes thicker, harder to homogenize, but the final samples are much more flexible and easily removable from their molds. Tensile strength is significantly reduced though.

Chitosan Series 4 - Adding of filler, panel design with two concentrations

  • Use of both magnetic (high chitosan concentration) and overhead stirrer (low chitosan concentration). Closed beaker used with the magnetic stirrer
  • Adding of insect frass to reduce shrinkage.
  • High concentration mix : 100 mL of water, 7 g of chitosan, 10 mL of acid acetic, 2.5 mL of glycerol, 2 g of frass.
  • High concentration mix : 200 mL of water, 10 g of chitosan, 20 mL of acid acetic, 5 mL of glycerol, 1 g of frass.
  • Design of a grid filled with two different concentrations.

Preliminary conclusions and way forward

The high concentration mix was oddly much more liquid and less viscous than the low one. This can be explained by the fact that the hight concentration mix was stirred in very stable condition (fixed speed using the magnetic stirrer and closed beaker avoiding water evaporation). Instead the low concentration mix was had evaporation issues and a large part of th mix was lost during a wrong manipulation of the beaker. Beside, only 9 grams were used instead of 10.
